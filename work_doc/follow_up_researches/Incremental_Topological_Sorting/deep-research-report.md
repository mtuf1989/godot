# Dynamic Topological Ordering Under Live Edge Updates

## Executive conclusion

For exact maintenance of a topological order under **edge insertions** after the Bender–Fineman–Gilbert–Tarjan line, the strongest sparse-graph result that still directly maintains a topological order is **Bhattacharya–Kulkarni (SODA 2020)**, with randomized expected total update time \(\tilde O(m^{4/3})\). It strictly improves the Bernstein–Chechik sparse result \(\tilde O(m\sqrt n)\) in the regime \(m=o(n^{3/2})\), which is exactly the regime that matters for sparse dependency graphs. In contrast, the major 2023 breakthrough by Chen et al. improves **incremental cycle detection** to \(O(m^{1+o(1)})\), but does **not** extend to maintaining a topological order, so it does not solve your core problem. citeturn20view0turn12search0turn13view0turn18view0

For **truly fully dynamic** exact maintenance with nontrivial update guarantees under both insertions and deletions, the only post-2015 paper I found that clearly crosses into this territory is **Karczmarz–Mukherjee–Sankowski (STOC 2022)**. They obtain the first subquadratic fully dynamic topological-order maintenance result for DAGs, with \(\tilde O(n^{1.529})\) worst-case update time, via reductions to dynamic path reporting / reachability in DAGs. The catch is that this is an algebraic, matrix-heavy, Monte Carlo data-structure result, not a clean combinatorial design likely to be pleasant on a production rendering thread. citeturn40search0turn41view2turn5view0

For your application profile — **sparse, small-to-medium DAGs** with \(V\approx 100\) to \(1000\), frequent wire connect/disconnect operations, immediate cycle rejection, and a **UI-blocking frame budget** — the literature points to a practical split. If you need the cleanest *exact* post-2015 engineering compromise, **Bernstein–Chechik 2018** is the better implementation target than Bhattacharya–Kulkarni 2020, even though BK20 has the better sparse asymptotic bound. If you can exploit temporal regularity or learn a decent approximate final order, **McCauley–Moseley–Niaparast–Singh (ICML 2024)** is the most compelling practical paper: their learned DFS variant is simple, preserves exact cycle detection / topological-order maintenance, and reports strong empirical wins on sparse real data. citeturn11view0turn12search0turn39search0turn19view1turn31view0

## State of the art after Bender

### Incremental sparse-DAG algorithms

**Bernstein–Chechik (SODA 2018)** is the first clear post-Bender sparse-graph improvement in this line. Their paper gives expected total update time \(O(m\sqrt n\log n)\) for incremental cycle detection and \(O(m\sqrt n\log n + n\sqrt n\log^2 n)\) for incremental topological sort. The high-level mechanism is a random sample of pivot vertices, sampled-ancestor / sampled-descendant signatures, and a bucketed ordered-list structure that keeps vertices grouped by sampled reachability statistics. The paper’s data structures include sampled reachability from every sampled vertex, \(O(n\log^2 n)\) bucket placeholders, and per-vertex memo sets used by the cycle detector; the result is mathematically strong, but already substantially more involved than the older local-reordering family. citeturn11view0turn33view1turn35view0turn33view2

**Bhattacharya–Kulkarni (SODA 2020)** is the current sparse-graph benchmark for exact incremental topological ordering and cycle detection. Their randomized algorithm achieves expected total update time \(\tilde O(m^{4/3})\), improving Bernstein–Chechik whenever \(m=o(n^{3/2})\). Technically, BK20 combines the Bernstein–Chechik sampling framework with the balanced-search ideas from Haeupler–Kavitha–Mathew–Sen–Tarjan, and the paper explicitly highlights that it is the first result to break the locality barrier \( \Omega(n\sqrt m)\) over a nontrivial sparsity range. That is genuine state-of-the-art theory for sparse DAGs, but it comes with a noticeably more elaborate partitioning-and-subgraph maintenance stack than BC18. citeturn20view0turn25view0turn41view1

A crucial caveat appears in **Bernstein–Dudeja–Pettie (ESA 2021)**. They observe that the new sparse topological-order results of **Bernstein–Chechik 2018** and **Bhattacharya–Kulkarni 2020** do **not** extend to maintaining SCCs in general directed graphs, and they close that SCC gap instead with an incremental Las Vegas algorithm of expected total update time \(\tilde O(m^{4/3})\). This matters for product design: if your system ever wants to *tolerate* cycles temporarily rather than reject them on insertion, then “maintain a vertex topological order” is the wrong abstraction; you must switch to **SCC maintenance plus topological order of the condensation DAG**. citeturn37view0

### Fully dynamic and deletion-aware results

The older incremental literature already points out the important special case for engineering: **deleting an edge never invalidates a current topological order**. Haeupler et al. explicitly note that topological-ordering algorithms can handle deletions because an arc deletion preserves topological order, although their *analysed* time bounds are incremental only. James Smith’s 2023 “robust topological orderings” preprint makes the same engineering observation and proposes a Pearce–Kelly-style representation that continues to track a “topological representation” even when cycles are tolerated, then re-examines cyclic edges after deletions. citeturn27search1turn26view0

That observation creates an important distinction for your environment. If a wire insertion that would create a cycle is **rejected immediately**, then your “fully dynamic DAG” problem is, in practice, **incremental maintenance plus trivial deletions**: insertions are the only operations that can force reordering, while accepted deletions are essentially housekeeping. If, on the other hand, the editor ever allows cyclic intermediate states, then you have crossed over into SCC maintenance or general reachability maintenance. citeturn27search1turn37view0

The only clearly post-2015 paper I found that gives a **direct, nontrivial fully dynamic topological-order maintenance guarantee** is **Karczmarz–Mukherjee–Sankowski (STOC 2022)**. They work in DAGs under fully dynamic single-edge updates that keep the graph acyclic and obtain \(\tilde O(n^{1+\rho}) = O(n^{1.529})\) worst-case update time for point-to-point path reporting; they then note that a fully dynamic topological order can be maintained in the same \(\tilde O(n^{1+\rho})\) worst-case time per update. The method is Monte Carlo randomised and is built on algebraic dynamic reachability / path-reporting machinery, which is theoretically impressive but far from the sort of low-constant, branch-predictable, adjacency-list code one usually wants on a synchronous UI path. citeturn40search0turn41view2turn5view0

### Prediction-augmented results

**McCauley–Moseley–Niaparast–Singh (ICML 2024)** is the first paper that directly couples **incremental topological ordering** with **algorithms-with-predictions**. It offers two results. The practical one, **Learned DFS Ordering**, has total running time \(O(m\eta+n)\), where \(\eta\) is the prediction error. The theory-heavy one, **Ideal Learned Ordering**, has total expected running time \(\tilde O(\min\{m\eta^{1/3},\, n\eta,\, n^2\})\) and is explicitly designed to be consistent, robust, and smooth with respect to prediction quality. Importantly, the ideal algorithm is not a wholly new combinatorial design: it decomposes the graph into subproblems and then runs the best-known sparse and dense worst-case algorithms — BK20 and BFGT15 — inside them. citeturn18view0turn19view0turn19view1turn17view2turn41view0

The empirical message of the ICML paper is unusually relevant to your setting. On sparse temporal datasets converted to DAG insertion sequences, they report strong improvements over natural DFS-style baselines. Their table includes datasets with 918 nodes / 12,320 static edges, 1,652 nodes / 9,790 static edges, and 14,839 nodes / 45,267 static edges, and they report that using only 5% training data can already produce one to two orders of magnitude improvement in processed work and up to a 6x runtime speed-up for LDFS over the tested baselines. That is one of the few pieces of direct empirical evidence in this research area; the paper itself remarks that prior empirical evidence for sophisticated topological-order data structures was limited. citeturn31view0turn31view1turn19view1turn19view2turn16view0

### Important near-misses

Two recent directions are intellectually important but do **not** directly solve exact topological-order maintenance. First, **Chen–Kyng–Liu–Meierhans–Probst Gutenberg (2023)** gives almost-linear time incremental **cycle detection** and SCC maintenance, but McCauley et al. explicitly note that these results do **not** extend to topological ordering. Second, the broader “dynamic graph algorithms with predictions” work of **van den Brand et al. (2023/2024)** studies predicted update sequences for dynamic graph problems such as transitive closure and directed cycle detection, but it does not give a direct topological-order maintenance structure of the kind you need, and its prediction interface is stronger and more sequence-specific than McCauley et al.’s per-vertex coarse predictions. citeturn13view0turn18view0turn28view0turn16view0

## Fully dynamic reality check

The most important architectural fact for a dependency editor is that **exact topological-order maintenance with immediate cycle rejection is not the same problem as maintaining arbitrary directed graphs under insertions and deletions**. If cycle-creating insertions are rejected synchronously, then deletions do not force any reordering at all; they simply remove constraints, leaving the current topological order valid. Under that policy, the correct theory lens is **incremental topological ordering with trivial deletions**, not general fully dynamic SCC maintenance. citeturn27search1turn26view0

This distinction is why the sparse incremental line is still the most relevant family for your application, even though you do have both connect and disconnect operations. Under a cycle-rejecting editor, BK20, BC18, and McCauley24 are all natural candidates. The moment you switch to a mode where cyclic states are allowed, you lose the right to speak about a vertex topological order and must instead maintain SCCs or a topological order of SCC components. The ESA 2021 SCC paper makes that boundary explicit and also makes clear that the best sparse topological-order algorithms do not automatically extend to SCC maintenance. citeturn37view0

The full-dynamic KMS22 result is therefore best interpreted as a **research ceiling** rather than a default engineering target. It proves that subquadratic worst-case fully dynamic topological-order maintenance is possible in DAGs, but it does so via dynamic path reporting and algebraic techniques whose implementation and constant factors are badly mismatched to a rendering-thread budget. The paper’s own statement places it in the world of dynamic reachability / path reporting, not in the world of lightweight local reordering. citeturn40search0turn41view2turn5view0

## Engineering assessment for sparse interactive graphs

For \(V\approx 100\) to \(1000\) and \(m=\Theta(n)\) to perhaps a small multiple of \(n\), the asymptotics become much less decisive than the literature tables make them look. Ignoring polylogarithmic factors, BK20’s sparse bound gives average work on the order of \(n^{1/3}\) per insertion when \(m=\Theta(n)\), while BC18 gives \(n^{1/2}\). At \(n=1000\), that is roughly a gap between about \(10\) and about \(31\) units of asymptotic work per insertion before constants; that is a real improvement, but not a dramatic one, especially when BK20 buys it using much richer partition maintenance and multi-phase searches. citeturn20view0turn25view0turn33view1turn24view0

That is why I would not choose BK20 as the **first implementation** for a production UI, despite its superior theorem. The algorithm maintains sampled-source reachability, vertex partitions \(V_{i,j}\), edge partitions \(E_{i,j}\), induced subgraphs \(G_{i,j}\), ordered lists, and multiple update phases; its proof structure gives exactly the feeling you would expect in practice — stronger asymptotics, but more moving parts, more bookkeeping, and more opportunities for hard-to-debug latency spikes. citeturn24view0turn25view0

**Bernstein–Chechik 2018** is a better “exact but still engineerable” compromise. It is not simple in the everyday sense, but it is materially cleaner than BK20, and its sampled-signature / bucket structure is easier to reason about in a small-engine codebase. It still provides immediate cycle detection at insertion time, and for \(n\le 1000\) the asymptotic gap to BK20 is small enough that constant factors and implementation clarity should matter more. The main downside is that, like BK20, it only controls **expected total** time, not per-frame tail latency. citeturn11view0turn35view0turn33view2

If you can exploit temporal regularity — and live node editors usually can, because users make small local perturbations to a mostly stable graph — then **McCauley et al.’s LDFS** is the strongest practical candidate in the post-2015 literature. Its maintained state is lightweight, its prediction interface is coarse and implementable, and its empirical section is directly on sparse real datasets. The paper shows substantial reductions both in processed work and in runtime, even with only 5% training data, and reports that LDFS remains robust when noise is added to the predictions. For a production dependency editor, “predictions” need not mean a learned model in the deep-learning sense; cached historical order, stable node positions, or running estimates of ancestor / descendant richness are all plausible sources of warm-start information. citeturn18view0turn19view0turn31view0turn31view1turn19view2

**Tail latency** is the decisive issue. None of BC18, BK20, or the McCauley structures give a compelling nontrivial **worst-case per-update** guarantee that I would trust on a strict synchronous frame budget. Their theorems are explicitly framed as total or expected total update time over a sequence, not deterministic per-operation caps. That makes them good at avoiding repeated full rebuilds in aggregate, but it does not guarantee that one unlucky wire drag will not trigger a disproportionately expensive local repair. The only paper in this set that directly addresses worst-case update tails is KMS22, and I do not view its algebraic machinery as a realistic frontend-thread choice. citeturn33view1turn25view0turn19view1turn41view2

## Recommendations

My recommendation depends on how hard your **tail-latency** constraint really is.

### If immediate cycle rejection is mandatory and the editor must stay acyclic

The best research-backed production choice is:

**Use an incremental algorithm, treat deletions as cheap, and do not chase fully dynamic algebraic machinery.** That framing matches both the dynamic-graph theory and your product semantics. citeturn27search1turn26view0

Within that framing, my ranked recommendations are:

**McCauley et al. 2024 LDFS** if you can supply stable predictions. It is the best match to sparse interactive graphs, has the cleanest practical data, and keeps the maintained state simple. This is the paper I would reach for first if your update streams are repetitive or user behaviour is stable enough that warm-starts are meaningful. citeturn19view0turn31view0turn31view1turn19view2

**Bernstein–Chechik 2018** if you want an exact, non-predictive, post-2015 algorithm with a better engineering profile than BK20. It gives you modern sparse-graph theory without the full complexity of BK’s partitioned balanced-search framework. For \(n\) in the low thousands or below, I believe this is the best exact “paper-to-code” compromise in the post-Bender line. citeturn11view0turn35view0turn33view2

**Bhattacharya–Kulkarni 2020** only if you want to optimise for the **best published sparse asymptotic** and are willing to spend significant implementation complexity budget to do it. It is the asymptotic winner, but not the engineering winner. citeturn20view0turn25view0turn24view0

### If worst-case per-update latency on the UI thread is non-negotiable

The literature does not offer a satisfying combinatorial answer. The only strong post-2015 worst-case option I found is **Karczmarz–Mukherjee–Sankowski 2022**, and I do **not** recommend it for a production rendering thread because the algorithmic machinery is too algebraic and too dense-state-heavy relative to your graph sizes and engineering constraints. If deterministic frame-budget compliance is a hard requirement, the honest conclusion is that the literature pushes you toward **architectural isolation** of updates rather than toward a pleasant exact fully dynamic topo-order data structure. citeturn41view2turn5view0turn40search0

### If the product may temporarily tolerate cycles

Do not try to “stretch” a topological-order data structure past its abstraction boundary. Switch to **SCC maintenance**. The closest relevant post-2015 result is **Bernstein–Dudeja–Pettie 2021** for incremental SCCs in sparse graphs, but I did not find a similarly clean and production-friendly exact **fully dynamic** SCC / topological-condensation maintenance result in this literature slice that I would recommend for your UI-thread use case. citeturn37view0turn40search0

## Open questions and limitations

A few gaps remain genuine research gaps, not gaps in this review. There is still no clearly dominant **combinatorial**, **fully dynamic**, **low-tail-latency** exact topological-order data structure that is both post-2015 and obviously implementable in a production dependency editor. The frontier after BK20 improved cycle detection much more than it improved exact topological-order maintenance, and the 2024 prediction-augmented work improved practice more than it improved deterministic worst-case tails. citeturn13view0turn18view0turn40search0

Also, the empirical literature is still thin. McCauley et al. explicitly note that empirical evidence for these higher-end dynamic topological-order structures was limited, and their paper is one of the few to report concrete runtime data on sparse real graphs. So the recommendation to prefer BC18 over BK20 for hand implementation is a theory-plus-engineering judgement, not a claim backed by a head-to-head production-grade benchmark suite between those two exact algorithms. citeturn16view0turn31view1

Finally, several papers do not state standalone big-\(O\) **space bounds** in theorem form, so some of the space / state-overhead discussion above is necessarily an implementation-oriented inference from the described maintained structures rather than a quoted theorem. I have called those cases out explicitly where they matter. citeturn35view0turn24view0turn5view0
